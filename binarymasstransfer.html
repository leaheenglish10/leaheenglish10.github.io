Abstract

Blue straggler stars are stars that appear to be younger and more luminous than expected. These stars are suspected to have been formed from a collision or mass transfer from a binary companion. WOCS 5379 is a binary star system in the NGC 188 star cluster that consists of a blue straggler star and a Helium white dwarf star orbiting each other (Gosnell et al. 2019). WOCS 14020 is a binary star system consisting of a blue lurker (a blue straggler that is harder to note on an HR diagram) and a white dwarf star in the open cluster M67 (Leiner et al. 2025). Using the stellar evolution code Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA) (Jermyn et al. 2023) , we model these systems to match the observed parameters of the systems. We model the future evolutionary trajectories of these binaries exploring two routes: conservative mass transfer, meaning the mass lost by the primary star is fully accreted onto the white dwarf, and non conservative mass transfer, meaning some percentage of mass lost by the primary is lost from the system and the rest is accreted onto the white dwarf. Fully conservative mass transfer leads to unstable mass transfer and causes a common envelope to form. Most non-conservative mass transfer led to the same conclusion, ending with a double white dwarf binary or double white dwarf merger. Though, highly non-conservative mass transfer can lead to stable mass transfer, and yields a model that looks broadly consistent with observed characteristics of symbiotic binaries. These results are interesting because the future evolution of blue straggler-white dwarf binary systems has not yet been modeled in detail, but they may ultimately form interesting systems such as symbiotic systems or double white dwarf mergers.
